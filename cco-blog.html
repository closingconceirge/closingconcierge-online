Listing Copy

5 MLS Write-Up Mistakes That Are Costing You Sales

By Closing Concierge Co. — Resources for Real Estate Agents

A strong listing description does not just describe a property. It creates a feeling strong enough to pull a buyer off the couch and into a showing. And yet, most MLS copy fails at the first hurdle. Here are the five mistakes we see most often, and what to do instead.

1. Leading with square footage instead of a feeling

The first line is your most valuable real estate on the page. When it opens with "Stunning 1,850 sq ft semi-detached," you have already wasted it. Buyers can see the square footage in the details. What they cannot see is how the home feels to live in. Start there instead. Give them a morning, an evening, a Sunday afternoon.

"The goal is not to describe the property. It is to make the buyer feel like they already live there."

2. Listing features instead of experiences

There is a meaningful difference between "updated kitchen with quartz countertops" and "a kitchen that was clearly made for someone who cooks." The first is a feature. The second is an invitation. Go through your copy and ask of every line: does this tell them what the room is, or what it is like to be in it?

3. Ignoring the neighbourhood entirely

Buyers are not just purchasing a property. They are purchasing a version of their daily life. The walk to the coffee shop, the school down the street, the trail that backs onto the ravine. If your listing copy ends at the front door, you are leaving some of the most compelling selling points unwritten.

4. Writing for search engines instead of people

Keyword stuffing is easy to spot and difficult to read. Buyers scroll fast. If your copy feels like a checklist, they move on. Write for the person first. A well-written description that keeps someone reading for thirty seconds will do more for you than a paragraph of stacked keywords.

5. Using the same template for every property

Buyers notice when copy feels recycled. Each property has something specific going for it, a detail, a light, a layout, a view, that makes it worth writing to. Take five minutes to find that thing before you start writing. It usually ends up being the sentence that books the showing.

Luxury Closings

Luxury Closing Gift Ideas for $1M+ Homes

By Closing Concierge Co. — Resources for Real Estate Agents

The closing gift is, in many ways, the punctuation at the end of the entire transaction. For buyers purchasing at the $1M+ level, it carries a certain weight. It should feel as considered and refined as the process itself. Here are ideas that land well at that price point, organized by category.

Artisan and Local

A beautifully curated basket from a local cheesemaker, winery, or specialty grocer never feels generic when it is done thoughtfully. The key is sourcing from somewhere specific to the area, something that says you know the community and you wanted to welcome them into it. A handwritten card matters more than the basket itself.

"The best closing gifts feel personal. Not expensive, not corporate. Personal."

Experience-Based Gifts

A reservation at a restaurant they have not tried yet. A private wine tasting. A weekend stay at a boutique inn nearby. Experiences tend to be remembered longer than objects, and they signal that you thought about who the client is, not just that you needed to give something.

Home and Entertaining

For a $1M+ purchase, a beautiful piece for the home can work exceptionally well when it is specific. A handcrafted serving board from a local maker. Custom linen napkins. A quality candle in a scent that reminds them of the neighbourhood. Avoid anything monogrammed unless you know them very well.

The Bespoke Closing Box

This is what we offer through the Luxe Collection: a fully assembled, branded closing experience curated to the client and the property. Every element is chosen intentionally, presented beautifully, and arrives feeling like it was made for them. It is the kind of gift that gets photographed and shared. Which, for your referral pipeline, is exactly the point.

What to Avoid

Generic gift cards, anything with your logo printed large on it, and anything that feels like it was ordered in bulk. At this price point, clients notice when something feels like a transaction rather than a gesture. The gift should feel like the end of a relationship that was worth having, because it probably was.

Client Experience

What Buyers Actually Notice First (And How to Write to It)

By Closing Concierge Co. — Resources for Real Estate Agents

There is a reason some listings get bookmarked and others get scrolled past in seconds. It is rarely about the photography. More often, it comes down to the first line of copy and whether it creates the right kind of pause.

The first five words carry most of the weight

Buyers move fast on platforms like Realtor.ca and MLS. The first few words of your listing description function almost like a headline. If those words are "Absolutely stunning home in," you have already told them nothing and used up your most valuable space. Lead with something specific, sensory, or surprising.

"Buyers are not reading your listing. They are scanning it for a reason to stop."

Emotion triggers the decision, logic justifies it

This is well-established in consumer psychology and it applies directly to real estate. The decision to book a showing is almost always emotional. The rational evaluation, the square footage, the commute distance, the school rating, comes after. Your copy should lead with emotion and let the details do the rest of the work lower on the page.

Specificity signals honesty

Vague superlatives like "gorgeous" and "beautiful" are invisible to buyers. They have read them too many times. Specific details, the coffee nook built into the kitchen island, the way the back deck faces south and catches the late afternoon, carry weight because they feel true. Specificity reads as honesty, and honesty builds the quiet confidence a buyer needs to take the next step.

End with momentum

The last line of your listing copy should nudge the reader forward. Not with a hard sell, but with a natural invitation. "This one books fast" or "Schedule a private showing before the weekend" does the job without being pushy. Give them somewhere to go.

Agent Branding

Why Your Listing Copy Is Also a Branding Statement

By Closing Concierge Co. — Resources for Real Estate Agents

Every piece of copy that goes out under your name is telling people something about who you are. The question is whether you are being intentional about it.

Sellers read your listings before they call you

This is one that surprises many agents. When a potential seller is evaluating who to list with, they often search your current listings and read the copy. They are not just looking at price points. They are looking at how you present properties, what kind of attention you pay to detail, and whether your writing suggests that you take the presentation of their home seriously. Weak copy can cost you a listing before you ever meet the client.

"Your MLS copy is often the first thing a potential seller sees. It should show them what you are like to work with."

Other agents notice too

The agents in your market are reading your listings. Referrals between agents are partly built on professional respect. The agent who consistently publishes polished, thoughtful copy is the one who earns a reputation for running a tight operation. That reputation compounds over time.

Consistent quality builds a consistent brand

You do not need to sound the same in every listing. Every property is different. But the level of care and craft in your copy should be consistent. That consistency, across every listing, every communication, every closing, is what builds a brand that people remember and refer.

You do not have to write it yourself

Many of the agents we work with are excellent at building relationships, reading markets, and closing deals. They are not necessarily writers, and that is perfectly fine. The work is separable. The brand benefit is the same either way.

Your listings should reflect the standard you hold yourself to.

Transaction Support

The Hidden Cost of Doing Everything Yourself

By Closing Concierge Co. — Resources for Real Estate Agents

Most agents track their income carefully. Far fewer track where their time actually goes. That gap is where a lot of potential revenue disappears quietly, without ever showing up on a report.

Time has a dollar value

If you bill effectively at $150 to $300 an hour in time spent with clients and in negotiation, then an hour spent writing listing copy, coordinating cleaning schedules, or sourcing closing gifts is an hour that cost you that rate. The math is rarely done explicitly, but it is always real. Outsourcing the tasks that someone else can do well is not a luxury. It is arithmetic.

"The agents who scale are not the ones who work harder. They are the ones who protect their time for the work only they can do."

The cognitive load is the part most people underestimate

It is not just the hours. It is the mental space. Thinking about what to write, what to order, how to word a client email, all of it occupies bandwidth that could be used for relationships and deals. Delegation is not just a time management strategy. It is a focus strategy.

What we take off your plate

Starting small is fine

You do not need to hand everything over at once. Most of our agents start with listing copy because it is the most visible and most time-consuming piece. They try one listing, see how it goes, and build from there. The free sample exists for exactly that reason.
